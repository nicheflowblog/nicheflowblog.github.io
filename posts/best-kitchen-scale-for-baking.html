What to Look For

Accuracy at the gram level: Any scale worth buying should measure in 0.1-gram increments (or at minimum, 1-gram increments). For baking, where the difference between 250g and 260g of flour can affect your results, this precision matters. If a scale only measures in 5-gram increments, keep looking.

Maximum weight capacity that fits your style: If you typically bake one cake at a time, 5 pounds is plenty. If you make multiple batches or bread dough, you'll want 8–11 pounds. The sweet spot for most home bakers is 5–8 pounds—enough capacity without the bulk of a commercial scale.

Tare function that actually works: Every scale should let you place your empty bowl on it and reset to zero. If you're doing step-by-step baking (adding flour, then sugar, then eggs), you need this feature to work smoothly. Cheap scales sometimes lag or fail when taring with heavier bowls, so this is worth testing before you buy.

Readable display and intuitive buttons: You'll use this scale multiple times per week, often with flour on your hands or while your eyes are focused on dough consistency. The display should be readable from across the counter, and buttons should be responsive without requiring extreme pressure. A scale that frustrates you gets used less often.

Our Verdict

For most home bakers, the Hario V60 Drip Scale delivers the performance you need at a price that doesn't sting. It's accurate, reliable, and makes a legitimate difference in your baking results. The built-in timer is the sort of thoughtful feature you don't realize you need until you have it.

If you're baking regularly—multiple times per week—the Escali Primo is worth the extra $15–20. The larger capacity means you won't need to weigh ingredients in batches, and the instant metric-to-imperial conversion saves time when switching between recipe sources.

The Acaia Pearl isn't essential unless you're baking professionally or want to track recipes with Bluetooth integration. It's exceptionally well-made, but it's also overkill for most home use.

Avoid scales that only measure in 5-gram increments, don't have manual tare buttons, or use outdated battery types that are hard to find. You'll end up frustrated and back in the market for a new scale within a year.
