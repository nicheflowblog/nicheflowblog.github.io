What to Look For

Capacity matters more than you think. For four people, you want at least 5.5 quarts, ideally 6 quarts or more. Anything smaller, and you'll be cooking in shifts during family dinners. The difference between a 4-quart and a 6-quart model sounds small, but it actually changes how often you'll use the machine. A larger basket also gives you more flexibility—more room for unevenly shaped foods like chicken breasts or whole vegetables.

Heating and air circulation consistency. Cheaper air fryers sometimes cook food unevenly, with one side overdone and the other pale. Look for models with heating elements positioned well and fans designed to move air throughout the entire basket. Reading customer reviews that specifically mention even cooking is more helpful than marketing claims. If possible, check YouTube videos of the model actually cooking food.

Control types and ease of use. Digital controls with presets are convenient, but simple mechanical dials work fine and have fewer things that can break. Think about what you actually want—if you're the type who buys a blender and uses three buttons, a complicated touchscreen air fryer might frustrate you. Simple is often better if it means you'll actually use the machine regularly.

Build quality and parts availability. A good air fryer should last 3–5 years with regular use. Baskets with properly bonded nonstick coating (not sprayed on) hold up longer. Before buying, check if replacement baskets and heating elements are available and reasonably priced. This matters more than brand name when something eventually wears out.
