What to Look For When Choosing a Stand Mixer

Motor Power Matters More Than You'd Think A mixer's wattage isn't everything, but it matters for consistency. Entry-level models (200–300 watts) handle cookies and cakes fine but may struggle with heavy bread doughs. If bread-making is important to you, aim for 400+ watts. Test this in real life: a 325-watt KitchenAid Artisan will outperform a 300-watt competitor because of engineering, but a 250-watt budget mixer won't match either.

Bowl Capacity and Tilt vs. Lift Design For most home bakers, 4–5 quarts is practical. A tilt-head bowl (like the Artisan) is easier to access and clean but slightly less stable for heavy mixing. Bowl-lift designs are more commercial-style and better for aggressive kneading. Both work fine for home use—this is preference territory. Most home recipes fit comfortably in a 5-quart bowl; go larger only if you regularly bake for crowds.

Attachment Versatility and Availability The paddle, dough hook, and whip are standard, but KitchenAid has the largest accessory ecosystem (pasta makers, food grinders, ice cream freezers). If you think you'll expand beyond basic mixing, this matters. Cheaper models often have fewer compatible attachments available. For pure baking, this is secondary—don't overpay for extras you won't use.

Noise Level and Durability Track Record Budget mixers are noticeably louder (like a blender at full blast). If you bake early mornings or have light-sleeping family members, this isn't trivial. Check owner reviews specifically for long-term reliability complaints. Some budget brands develop motor issues after 2 years; established brands like KitchenAid and Hamilton Beach have better track records. Spending $150 more for a 5-year lifespan instead of 2 years is actually economical.
