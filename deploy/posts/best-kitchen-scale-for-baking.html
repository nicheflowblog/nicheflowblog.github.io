What to Look For

Accuracy and Precision
For baking, you need a scale that measures down to at least 1 gram, ideally 0.1 grams. This matters most for smaller batches and when using ingredients like yeast, baking soda, or specialty flours. Look for scales that specify their margin of error—anything within 2 grams is acceptable for home use. Avoid scales that only measure in half-ounce increments; they're too imprecise for reliable baking.

Capacity and Platform Size
A 5-11 pound capacity covers most baking scenarios. For bread dough or large batches, you want room to spare. The platform itself should be large enough to accommodate your bowl comfortably. Some scales have small platforms that make it difficult to balance mixing bowls, which defeats the purpose of using a scale in the first place.

Display and Interface
You want quick, clear readouts. Look for backlit displays if you have dim kitchen lighting, and make sure the tare (zero) button is easy to find and use. You'll be using tare constantly—resetting the scale to zero after placing your bowl so you can add ingredients one at a time. An unresponsive button or confusing interface gets old fast.

Durability and Build Quality
Kitchen scales get spills and daily wear. Stainless steel platforms withstand damage better than plastic. Check the battery accessibility too—you don't want a scale that requires disassembly to change batteries. Scales in the $25-$50 range generally last 3-5 years with normal use; premium models often last longer and handle more abuse.

The scales you should skip are those that only measure in ounces (bakers need grams), wireless models with smartphone apps (this feature adds cost and complexity without benefit), and scales with capacities below 3 pounds (they're too limited for typical recipes).

One practical tip: whichever scale you choose, practice using it a few times with everyday cooking before diving into precision baking. Get comfortable with the tare button and how the scale responds. Most baking failures aren't caused by the scale—they're caused by not using it correctly. Once you develop the habit of weighing ingredients, your baking consistency will improve noticeably.
