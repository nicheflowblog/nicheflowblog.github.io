What to Look For

Basket Size Matters More Than You Think: A 2.5-quart basket is genuinely limiting if you're cooking for more than two people regularly. You'll be doing multiple batches, which means cold food while waiting for the next round. For solo cooking or meal-prepping single portions, compact is fine. For anyone else, aim for at least 4 quarts. Check the actual usable space—some manufacturers use gross capacity that includes the heating element area.

Heating Speed and Consistency: Budget air fryers vary wildly in how evenly they heat. I've tested models where one side of the basket gets crispy while the other stays pale. The best sub-$100 options reach 400°F in under 5 minutes and distribute heat evenly across the basket. Dial-based models (mechanical thermostats) are often more reliable than budget digital displays, though they're less precise.

Ease of Cleaning: This isn't glamorous but it's crucial for whether you'll actually use the thing. Non-stick coatings degrade differently—some hold up after a year, others flake after three months. Baskets that fit in the dishwasher are worth the extra $10-20 if budget allows. Avoid models with crevices where oil pools and oxidizes.

Controls and Usability: Digital displays look fancy but are often the first things to fail on budget models. A simple mechanical dial with a timer that actually rings when it goes off is more reliable than a display that freezes mid-cook. The difference between a good button layout and a confusing one shows itself within the first week—make sure you can adjust temperature and time without constantly consulting the manual.
