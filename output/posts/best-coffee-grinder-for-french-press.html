What to Look For in a French Press Grinder

Burr Type Over Blade: Burr grinders—whether conical or flat—produce uniform particle size that blade grinders can't match. This consistency is non-negotiable for French press, where uneven grinds create muddy, over-extracted coffee. Blade grinders may be cheaper, but they waste beans and deliver disappointment in every cup.

Grind Setting Range: You need a grinder with at least 30–40 settings, with dedicated coarse positions. French press requires grinds noticeably coarser than drip coffee—essentially peppercorn-sized particles. Grinders with only 10–15 settings often lack true coarse options and force compromises that undermine extraction.

Heat Management: Grinding generates friction. Cheaper motors with metal burrs running at high speed can heat beans above 100°F, damaging volatile flavor compounds before brewing even starts. Look for grinders specifically designed with thermal considerations—mentioned in product documentation or trusted reviews. Brands like Baratza and Breville engineer this in; budget models often don't.

Consistency and Fines Control: Even "coarse" grinds contain some ultra-fine powder particles. High-quality grinders minimize fines because they clog French press filters and create sediment. If you can find grinder reviews mentioning fines testing, prioritize those with demonstrably lower fines percentages—typically under 8% for quality burr grinders.

Our Verdict

French press coffee thrives on consistency, and the grinder matters more than most people realize. For the majority of home brewers, the Baratza Encore represents the genuine best value—it costs less than specialty coffee for a month, produces reliable coarse grinds for years, and removes the guesswork from dialing in extraction. If you're grinding every morning and speed matters, the Virtuoso+ is worth the $5–10 upgrade for noticeably faster operation.

Budget shoppers shouldn't assume blade grinders are acceptable; the Capresso Infinity proves that burr technology exists at accessible prices. Step up only to Breville or Wilfa if you're brewing multiple ways or have experienced enough French press coffee to appreciate marginal consistency improvements—both are excellent, but overkill for single-method brewers.

One final note: no grinder performs its best without clean-out between sessions. Even quality burr grinders accumulate old bean oils that go rancid. Spend $5 on grinder cleaning pellets and use them monthly. This simple maintenance extends your grinder's life and ensures every cup tastes fresh.
