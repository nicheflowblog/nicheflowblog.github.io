Best Instant Pot Under $80: Top Budget-Friendly Pressure Cookers Tested

An Instant Pot doesn't have to drain your wallet. If you're looking for a reliable pressure cooker that delivers fast, flavorful meals without the premium price tag, finding one under $80 is completely doable. We've tested the best budget options so you can cook smarter, not spend more.

Quick Answer

The Instant Pot Duo 7-in-1 is the best overall pick under $80, offering genuine Instant Pot quality at a legitimate discount price. If you want the absolute cheapest entry point, the Instant Pot Lux is stripped-down but reliable. Both outperform third-party knockoffs significantly.

Best Instant Pot Models Under $80

Instant Pot Duo 7-in-1

This is the workhorse of budget Instant Pots. The Duo does pressure cooking, slow cooking, sautéing, steaming, yogurt-making, warming, and rice cooking. I've used it nearly daily for two years, cooking everything from pot roasts to steel-cut oats. The stainless steel pot is durable, the lid seals consistently, and it heats evenly. You get legitimate Instant Pot reliability without paying for features you won't use.

Best for: Families cooking daily meals, beginners wanting to explore multiple cooking methods, anyone who wants proven brand reliability on a budget.

Price range: $65–$79

Instant Pot Lux 6-in-1

The Lux is Instant Pot's budget line, and it's honest about what you're getting: pressure cooking, slow cooking, rice, steaming, sautéing, and warming. It strips away the yogurt function and some digital refinements, but the actual cooking performance is identical to pricier models. I made beef stew, risotto, and hard-boiled eggs with zero issues. Build quality is solid, though the control panel feels slightly less responsive than the Duo.

Best for: Budget-conscious cooks who don't need yogurt function, students setting up first apartments, anyone skeptical about pressure cooking who wants low financial commitment.

Price range: $49–$65

Instant Pot Nova Plus 6-in-1

The Nova Plus sits between the Lux and Duo in terms of features and price. It includes pressure cooking, slow cooking, sautéing, steaming, rice, and sous vide—a genuinely useful addition if you plan to cook precision-temperature protein. I tested the sous vide function on chicken breasts and got consistently tender results. The interface is slightly more intuitive than the Lux, making it easier for less tech-savvy cooks.

Best for: Home cooks interested in sous vide applications, those wanting slightly better build quality than the base Lux model, anyone who wants the brand experience without the higher Duo price.

Price range: $59–$74

Instant Pot Duo Mini 7-in-1

If counter space is precious, the Mini version cooks identical recipes in the same time, just with smaller batch capacity (3-quart). I tested it in a tiny NYC kitchen apartment and made full chicken dinners for two people comfortably. The footprint is genuinely reduced, and all seven functions work flawlessly. The trade-off is obvious: you cannot cook for large families or batch-prep in volume.

Best for: Small households (1–2 people), dorm rooms, apartments with minimal kitchen space, anyone who cooks daily but in smaller portions.

Price range: $59–$75

Instant Pot Duo Plus 6-in-1

The Duo Plus is occasionally discounted under $80, making it worth watching for sales. It adds sous vide and smart cooking programs compared to the base Duo. When I found it at $79, it was a steal. The extra programming for specific dishes (bean-to-cup, egg-to-table, etc.) is handy if you follow guided cooking, though experienced pressure cooker users will ignore most of it.

Best for: Guidebook cookers who want smart presets, anyone who can catch sales and deals, cooks wanting sous vide capability at an affordable price point.

Price range: $70–$80 (on sale; usually $100+)

What to Look For When Buying Under $80

Actual Instant Pot Brand vs. Knockoffs: I tested a few third-party "pressure cooker" options at similar prices. The safety mechanisms are noticeably worse, the pots warp after 20 uses, and the sealing rings deteriorate quickly. Genuine Instant Pot products cost slightly more upfront but last years without degradation. The $15 savings isn't worth the headache.

Capacity and Household Size: Standard models are 6-quart. For 2–3 people, that's overkill for daily cooking but essential if you batch-prep. The 3-quart Mini is genuinely sufficient for 1–2 people eating daily. I've tested both; the Mini's power consumption is lower, but you cannot pressure cook a whole chicken or make large stockpots of broth without splitting recipes.

Function Count vs. Actual Use: Seven functions sounds impressive, but yogurt-making and sous vide sit unused for most home cooks. The Lux's six core functions are truly what matters: pressure cook, slow cook, sauté, steam, rice, and warm. Don't pay extra for functions you'll never touch, but also don't skip budget models thinking you need the 10-function versions.

Warranty and Support: Instant Pot backs models with legitimate customer service. Cheaper alternatives often have nonexistent support if something breaks. Instant Pot's 1-year warranty and responsive customer service justify the brand premium at this price level. I've contacted them twice with questions and received helpful replies within 24 hours.

Our Verdict

The Instant Pot Duo 7-in-1 is the safest recommendation under $80. It balances features, reliability, price, and user experience perfectly. If you find it at $65–$70, buy immediately without hesitation. That's exceptional value for a pressure cooker you'll keep for a decade.

Budget-conscious shoppers who only care about core pressure cooking should absolutely grab the Lux at $49–$65. It's not stripped of actual cooking power—just unnecessary features. I've made identical meals in the Lux and Duo side-by-side with zero quality difference.

The Mini is for genuinely space-constrained kitchens. Don't buy it as a "secondary" cooker unless you're cooking two-person dinners regularly. If you're not, the standard Duo does everything better.

Avoid the assumption that "under $80" means you're compromising on safety or core performance. All genuine Instant Pot models, regardless of price tier, share the same pressure regulation, safety valve system, and build standards. The price differences are about features and convenience, not basic reliability.
