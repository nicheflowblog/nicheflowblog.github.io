What to Look For

True Convection vs. Convection Assist: This distinction matters more than marketing suggests. "True convection" includes a heating element dedicated to the convection fan, ensuring consistent heat circulation. "Convection assist" relies on the standard heating elements plus a fan, which works adequately for most tasks but heats less evenly. Under $150, expect convection assist from most brands—it's still notably better than standard toaster ovens, but don't expect professional-grade results.

Capacity and Size: A 4-slice capacity (typically 0.6–0.8 cubic feet) handles most household needs. If you frequently cook for four or more people or want to roast full-sized chicken pieces, stretch toward the larger end of your budget. However, remember that larger ovens have slower preheat times and use more counter space. Measure your intended location before purchasing.

Temperature Control Method: Digital displays are convenient and more precise, but analog dials (knobs and switches) are more durable long-term. Under $150, digital models occasionally develop glitchy displays, while analog controls rarely fail. Consider your tolerance for potential replacement—if you want a five-year investment, analog edges slightly ahead; if you're comfortable with replacements every 2–3 years, digital offers superior usability.

Interior Light and Easy Cleaning: LED interior lights prevent you from opening the door repeatedly (maintaining temperature and saving energy). Nonstick or light-colored interiors show spills more obviously, prompting timely cleaning—dark interiors hide mess but make it harder to spot buildup. Both are acceptable; choose based on your cleaning habits. Removable crumb trays are non-negotiable; models without them accumulate debris that's nearly impossible to access.
